AFL is a dynamic sport unlike any other in the world, where the bottom team is always a chance to upset the top team in any given week. Every team has a story each season, with a mix of highs and lows. Explore the statistics which have defined your club's history

Round by Round

Clubs go through high and low patches throughout any season. There are a number of reasons for this, as explored in the charts below.

In Richmond's 2017 premiership year, they were dominant for most of the season. However, they did get beaten by nearly 70 points on two seperate occasions, one of which was against Adelaide, who they defeated in the Grand Final.

Player height is important for several reasons. AFL involves lots of aerial contests and marks where being taller in benefical for marking the ball; however, smaller players tend to be faster and more skillfull.

In Richmond's 2017 premiership year the fielded team for the majority of games was smaller than the opposition by several centimeters on average. This suited the Tiger's gamestyle, which relyed on frenetic pressure.

Syndney similarly fielded a smaller team much of 2012-2016, in which they won a premiership and finsihed top of the ladder on two occasions, indicating that smaller sides often have greater success

Team age is another important factor, with younger teams often having greater stamina but older teams havign more experiance and game knowldege.

Richmond had a relatively junior side for the majority of their 2017 season, only fielding a more experianced side on four occasions during the season.

Converesly, Hawthorn fielded a more experianced side for all but two games in 2013 and one game in 2015, both of which they won premierships.

From all the data collected between 2012-2020, trends in which teams have been most succesful emerge.

Hawthorn, Geelong and Sydney can be seen to be the most successful teams in this period, with each only having two other teams which have a greater avergae margin against them.


Conversely, Gold Coast, Melbourne and Carlton have been the least successful teams, only having postive average margins against 1, 2 and 3 teams respectively.

The largest margin recorded from 2012-2020 was Hawthorn's 162 point win over GWS in 2012. This is not suprising, as GWS was only introduced to the competition that year and Hawthorn finnished top of the ladder

Rainfall

"Wet Weather footy" is a term thrown around alot, describing the different playstyle required to handle a slippery ball.

During their premiership streak, Richmond were renowned for their ability to play in the wet. This trend can be seen in their 2017 season, where their average margin increased as the rainfall did.

Venue

Home ground advantage is often a factor in sports discussion across the world. This is particularly true for AFL, where not only do you have the majority of the fans but also are used to the pitch, as not all AFL playing surfaces have the same dimensions

Geelong has its home ground at Kardinia Park and has the highest avergae margin at a home ground in the competion, with not a single other team having a postive margin at that ground

Interestingly, 8 of the 18 teams have a negative margin at their Home ground. This may be because several teams share home grounds.

Ladder

AFL teams play 23 regular games to determine their ladder position at the end of the season. The top 8 teams play in the finals, with the higher ranked teams receiving advantages like extra chance games or home ground.
However, success is measured by a team's ability to win the grand final and be crowned the PREMIER team.

The dominant teams of the last few years include Hawthorn , who won three premierships from 2013-2015, and Richmond , who also won three premierships from 2017-2020.

One of the biggest underdog stories in recent history was the Western Bulldogs premiership win from 7th postion, the lowest position in AFL hisotry for a team to win from.

Interestingly, only one team who finished on top of the laddder at the end of the regular season went on to win the grand final, that being Hawthorn in 2013.

Looking at Richmond's 2017 premiership season, their unqiue style of play is reflected in their abnormal stats.
Despite finishing 3rd and winning the premiership that year, Richmind ranked 15th for total disposals and 16th for marks, two of the key stats of the game that indicate how much of the ball the team has.

Even more suprising is their ball use when they do have it, ranking worst in disposal efficiency and also racking up the most turnovers and clangers (which refers to unforced errors) in the competition.
This summarises their gamestyle, which revolved around chaotic pressure and surging the ball forward and won them three premierships

An obvious trend of high scores resulting in more wins emerges from the data. Some interesting cases are Richmond, who only recorded the 8th highest score and finished 3rd, and Brisbane, who actaully scored the 11th highest in the competition but finnished last

Converesly, defensive tackle and intercept stats also correlate with success. Richmond were the best team at tackles inside-50 and intercept possesions, reflecting their high pressure gamestyle.

An interesting observation is Richmond's AFL Fantasy and Supercoach points, two systems which attempt to quantitatively rate each player's gameday performance.
Richmond is ranked rather low in these metrics, even been ranked as the second worst team by AFL Fantasy points. This is despite finishing 3rd and winning the premiership.
Similarly, Collingwood are ranked the highest by AFL Fantasy despite finishing 13th, highlighting how fast the game is evolving and the measures of success are changing.
